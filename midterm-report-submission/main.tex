\begin{abstract}
We present a decision-support system for the tabletop war game
\emph{Axis \& Allies Pacific 1940} that converts a single photograph
of a contested territory into three tactical outputs: a predicted attacker
win probability, an optimal unit-composition recommendation, and a
deterministic combat simulation.
The perception pipeline combines an open-vocabulary object detector
(\mbox{OWL-ViT}~\cite{owlvit}) with a fine-tuned Vision
Transformer~\cite{vit} classifier and a colour-based faction heuristic.
Downstream reasoning is handled by a learned MLP surrogate trained on
Monte-Carlo battle outcomes, together with an exhaustive IPC-budget search
and a rules-based combat simulator.
The full system runs end-to-end on CPU with a \mbox{FastAPI} backend
and a static HTML frontend.
On a held-out evaluation set the MLP win-rate predictor matches the
Monte-Carlo simulator to within $0.34$ percentage points
($\mathrm{MAE}\!=\!0.0034$ over $500$ scenarios at $5\!\times\!10^{4}$
rollouts each); a Bayesian linear-regression head distilled from the
MLP has additionally been prototyped for calibrated composition
ranking.
\red{Quantitative evaluation of detection and classification on the
held-out board photographs is ongoing and will be reported in the
final submission.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\emph{Axis \& Allies Pacific 1940} is a hex-and-counter war game whose
combat resolution is fully governed by published probability tables:
each unit type attacks and defends on a fixed dice value, casualties
are removed in a prescribed order, and rounds repeat until one side is
eliminated.
In principle this makes the game an ideal target for automated
decision-support: every question of the form \emph{``given this stack,
what is my win probability?''} has a closed-form Monte-Carlo answer.
In practice, players rarely use such tools at the table because the cost
of manually entering every unit count into a web calculator outweighs
the informational benefit.

Our project closes that gap. A player takes a single, uncontrolled
photograph of a contested territory; our system detects the plastic
miniatures in the image, classifies each one by unit type and
faction colour, and immediately returns (i)~a predicted win
probability for the named attacker, (ii)~the optimal attacker force
under two different IPC~budgets, and (iii)~a fully simulated battle
with surviving unit counts on each side.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
(i)~an end-to-end pipeline from noisy board photo to tactical
recommendation, running on CPU; (ii)~open-vocabulary detection
requiring no domain-specific bounding-box labels; (iii)~a
fine-tuned ViT classifier for seven unit types; (iv)~an MLP
win-rate surrogate paired with exhaustive IPC-budget search; and
(v)~a deterministic rules-based combat simulator that doubles as
the MLP's training-data generator.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Open-vocabulary detection and ViT classification.}
OWL-ViT~\cite{owlvit} accepts free-text category names at inference
time, removing the need for domain-specific bounding-box training.
We use \texttt{google/owlvit-large-patch14} through HuggingFace
Transformers for its reliability on small, overlapping figurines
without fine-tuning. For classification we fine-tune
\texttt{vit\_small\_patch16\_224}~\cite{vit} from \texttt{timm}~\cite{timm};
the small variant is CPU-friendly and sufficient for a 7-class
problem.

\paragraph{Game-state recognition from photographs.}
LiveChess2FEN~\cite{livechess2fen} is the closest prior art,
classifying chess pieces in cropped squares from overhead
photographs. Our task is harder --- pieces are three-dimensional,
stacked, and shot at oblique angles --- but the core insight
(detection then crop-classification) is the same.

\paragraph{Game AI: RL vs.\ surrogate distillation.}
Self-play RL agents reach superhuman play on board and video
games~\cite{alphazero} but require millions of
episodes and produce opaque policies. We instead distil the
published combat rules into a small MLP that predicts
$P(\text{attacker wins})$ in one forward pass, replacing
$2\,000$ Monte-Carlo rolls per query. Existing hobbyist tools
(\emph{AAOddsCalc}, \emph{TripleA}) serve a similar purpose via
direct simulation but require manual data entry and offer no
composition recommendation.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

Three datasets feed the system, each with different provenance.

\paragraph{Real board photographs.}
Thirteen JPG images in \texttt{app/backend/test/} were captured
during actual play sessions under varying illumination and camera
angles. These are held out from all training and used
only for end-to-end evaluation of the perception pipeline; one
representative example is reproduced in
Figure~\ref{fig:method_detection} with the detection + classification
overlay applied.

\paragraph{Cropped unit instances.}
A labelled set of cropped per-piece images was collected and used to
fine-tune \texttt{vit\_small\_patch16\_224} end-to-end (the deployed
\texttt{vit\_classifier.pth} loads with \texttt{pretrained=False},
confirming that the backbone --- not just a linear head --- is
trained). Figure~\ref{fig:crops} shows the twelve crops produced
from Figure~\ref{fig:method_detection} together with the ViT
confidence (operating threshold $\!\geq\!0.7$ matches
Section~\ref{sec:classify}) and the per-faction HSV pixel counts
driving the faction assignment.
\red{Per-class crop counts, collection protocol, and train/val
split are not in the public repository and will be reported in the
final submission.}

\paragraph{Simulator-generated battles.}
We generated $N\!=\!100\,000$ training samples by stratified
sampling of unit-count ranges --- $40\%/40\%/20\%$ uniform over
$[0,3]/[0,10]/[0,30]$ --- to balance small skirmishes against the
larger battles a player is more likely to encounter.
Each sample is a 15-d unit-count vector; the label is the empirical
attacker win rate over $B\!=\!2\,000$ simulator rollouts.
The dataset is stored as a compressed NumPy archive
(\texttt{.npz}); the trained checkpoint is
\texttt{winrate\_model.pt} ($1.4$~MB).

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Scope refinement from the proposal.}
The proposal framed perception as a whole-board ViT-vs-CNN encoder;
we instead pivoted to open-vocabulary detection followed by
per-piece classification, which removes the need for manual
bounding-box annotation and exposes intermediate unit counts the
user can audit before reasoning runs. The pivot keeps the
proposal's ``transformer for visual game-state'' hypothesis at the
per-piece level (OWL-ViT detector $+$ ViT-Small classifier) rather
than the whole-board level; we therefore do not separately train a
CNN baseline. The downstream stack is unchanged, with the proposed
Bayesian linear-regression layer (Section~\ref{sec:blr}) prototyped
in isolation. The originally proposed React frontend was
implemented as a static HTML$+$JS page since the feature set did
not justify the framework.

The system is a sequential pipeline of five stages
(Figure~\ref{fig:overview}) operating on the eight-class unit
vocabulary in Table~\ref{tab:units}, which encodes the official cost
and dice statistics from the rulebook.
Of the eight unit types, seven appear on the attacking side; the
anti-aircraft (\textsc{aa}) gun is defender-only by the rules.
The ViT classifier therefore covers the seven attacker-eligible
classes (detection itself is class-agnostic; see
Section~\ref{sec:detect}), while the simulator and MLP both consume
the full eight-class vocabulary --- which is why the MLP input vector
is fifteen-dimensional (seven attacker counts plus all eight defender
counts).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\caption{Unit vocabulary modelled by the system. Attack and defence
values are out of six (\emph{Axis \& Allies} uses d6 dice).
\textsc{AA} is defender-only.}
\label{tab:units}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrc@{}}
\toprule
Code & Unit & IPC & Atk\,/\,Def \\
\midrule
\texttt{Infantry}  & Infantry             &  3 & 1\,/\,2 \\
\texttt{Mech}      & Mechanised infantry  &  4 & 1\,/\,2 \\
\texttt{Artillery} & Artillery            &  4 & 2\,/\,2 \\
\texttt{Tank}      & Tank                 &  6 & 3\,/\,3 \\
\texttt{Fighter}   & Fighter aircraft     & 10 & 3\,/\,4 \\
\texttt{TacBmb}    & Tactical bomber      & 11 & 3\,/\,3 \\
\texttt{StrBmb}    & Strategic bomber     & 12 & 4\,/\,1 \\
\texttt{AA}        & Anti-aircraft gun    &  5 & ---\,/\,1 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Detection (open-vocabulary, class-agnostic)}
\label{sec:detect}

OWL-ViT is used purely as a class-agnostic \emph{localiser}: we
query the model with a single fixed list of seven broad text prompts
(\emph{``plastic military figurine toy''}, \emph{``plastic tank
toy''}, \emph{``plastic artillery toy''}, $\ldots$) chosen for high
recall on the visual classes of plastic pieces, and discard the
per-prompt labels after post-processing.
Only the bounding boxes are passed downstream; the unit class is
assigned later by the ViT classifier of
Section~\ref{sec:classify}.
Detections below confidence $\tau{=}0.05$ are discarded; the
remainder are passed through (a)~NMS at IoU $0.1$, (b)~a containment
filter that removes boxes fully enclosed by a higher-scoring
neighbour, and (c)~area and aspect-ratio sanity checks
($0.001 \leq A_{\text{box}}/A_{\text{img}} \leq 0.4$,
$\text{AR} \leq 10$).

\subsection{Classification (fine-tuned ViT)}
\label{sec:classify}

Each surviving bounding box is cropped, resized to $224{\times}224$,
and normalised with ImageNet statistics before being passed through
a \texttt{vit\_small\_patch16\_224} backbone with a custom 7-way
linear head.
Predictions below confidence $0.7$ are discarded.

\subsection{Faction Assignment (HSV heuristic)}
\label{sec:faction}

Each crop is converted to HSV; pixels in the Japan-orange band
($H\!\in\![5,20]$) or USA-green band ($H\!\in\![25,45]$) are
counted and the piece is assigned to whichever has more.
This five-line OpenCV step is parameter-free.
Pieces in other colours (UK, ANZAC, China) fall outside both bands
and are dropped, so the current scope is JP vs.\ US only.

\subsection{Win-Rate Prediction (MLP surrogate)}
\label{sec:mlp}

The 15-d unit-count vector is fed to an MLP with hidden widths
$256\!\to\!256\!\to\!128\!\to\!64$ (BatchNorm and ReLU after the
first three layers), followed by a $64\!\to\!1$ sigmoid head that
estimates $P(\text{attacker wins})$. We train with AdamW
(lr~$=\!10^{-4}$, weight decay $0.05$,
BCE loss) for $70\,000$ iterations on the simulator-generated
dataset of Section~\ref{sec:data}; inputs are standardised with
training-set statistics saved alongside the weights.

\subsection{Best-Composition Recommendation}
\label{sec:recommend}

Given a fixed defender and budget $B$, we enumerate every attacker
mix with total IPC $\leq B$ ($O(10^{4})$ candidates per budget) and
score each with a separate MLP forward pass --- the dominant
latency cost in \texttt{/recommend}
(Section~\ref{sec:profiling}). The recommender runs twice: once at
the attacker's own IPC value (``what should I have brought?'') and
once at the defender's (``what is optimal at their investment
level?'').

\subsection{Bayesian Composition Ranking (prototyped, not deployed)}
\label{sec:blr}

For calibrated ranking with explicit uncertainty, we have
additionally prototyped a
Bayesian linear regression (BLR) head distilled from the MLP, on a
hand-crafted 28-d feature map of counts, force ratios, and pairwise
interactions. It is fit online from sufficient statistics, with the
posterior sampled by Monte Carlo to produce an upper-confidence
acquisition score. On the held-out set it matches MLP accuracy
while running in $<\!1$\,ms per query, evaluating $N\!=\!8\,000$
candidate mixes in under one second versus the $\sim\!5.6$\,s
MLP-direct baseline.
\red{Production integration into \texttt{/recommend} remains as
future work beyond the final-report timeline.}

\subsection{Combat Simulation (rules-based)}
\label{sec:sim}

A deterministic simulator implements the official combat sequence:
\emph{(i)} one round of AA pre-fire (each AA rolls up to three shots
at $1/6$, capped by attacker aircraft; casualties hit
Fighter\,$\to$\,TacBmb\,$\to$\,StrBmb);
\emph{(ii)} simultaneous combat rounds at the attack/defence values
of Table~\ref{tab:units}, with artillery boosting paired infantry
from $1$ to $2$ and a TacBmb $+1$ when accompanied by a Fighter or
Tank, casualties applied land-first-then-air
(infantry\,$\to$\,art\,$\to$\,tank\,$\to$\,ftr\,$\to$\,tac\,$\to$\,str)
and Mech merged into infantry for selection;
\emph{(iii)} termination when one side has no remaining land or air
units.
The simulator is vectorised in NumPy ($B\!=\!2\,000$
parallel battles per call, RNG seeded for reproducibility) and
serves both \texttt{/simulate} and the MLP's training-data
generator.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Inference Profiling (complete)}
\label{sec:profiling}

We built a micro-benchmark script
(\texttt{app/backend/bench\_inference.py}) and measured wall-clock
time for each pipeline stage on a Windows CPU laptop
(Table~\ref{tab:latency}).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{3pt}
\caption{Per-stage CPU latency (Windows laptop, no GPU).}
\label{tab:latency}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrl@{}}
\toprule
Stage & Latency & Notes \\
\midrule
OWL-ViT detection      & $\sim$5.0\,s   & dominates \texttt{/analyze} \\
ViT classification     & $\sim$130\,ms  & 1.5$\times$ faster batched \\
MLP single forward     & $\sim$0.28\,ms & per \texttt{/winrate} call \\
MLP batch ($10^{4}$)   & $\sim$3.8\,ms  & $\approx$700$\times$ kernel speed-up \\
\texttt{find\_best\_attack} & $\sim$2.8\,s & per budget; $2{\times}$ per call \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The dominant bottleneck in \texttt{/recommend} is the sequential
MLP calls in \texttt{find\_best\_attack}: batching all $10^{4}$
candidates into one forward pass shrinks the MLP-only cost from
$\sim\!2.8$\,s to $\sim\!3.8$\,ms ($\sim\!700{\times}$ kernel
speed-up; $\sim\!112{\times}$ wall-clock when the two-budget loop
is included). The same profiling identifies further engineering opportunities
(OWL-ViT text-embedding caching, input downscaling, batched ViT
classification, and \texttt{asyncio} offload of \texttt{/analyze})
which are out of scope for this submission.

\subsection{Planned Evaluations \planned}
\label{sec:planned-eval}
\red{Two evaluations remain for the final submission.
\textbf{Perception accuracy on held-out photos}: a single
hand-labelling pass over the $13$ photos yields detection
precision/recall against OWL-ViT output and per-class top-1 plus a
confusion matrix on the predicted ViT classes.
\textbf{Recommendation quality}: for each held-out board we
simulate $10^{3}$ battles for the originally fielded mix and the
system's recommended mix at the same IPC budget, and report the
mean win-rate delta and the fraction of boards on which the
recommendation strictly improves. This experiment also discharges
the end-to-end evaluation promised in the feasibility report.}

\subsection{Win-Rate Calibration (preliminary)}
\label{sec:winrate-calib}
On $500$ held-out scenarios with $5\!\times\!10^{4}$ rollouts each
as ground truth, the MLP achieves $\mathrm{MAE} = 0.0034$
(\emph{i.e.}, $\sim\!0.34$ percentage points). Four representative
cases (Table~\ref{tab:mlp-rep}) all track the simulator within half
a percentage point.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have demonstrated a functioning end-to-end pipeline that converts
a photograph of a board-game position into three forms of tactical
decision support.
The perception and serving infrastructure are complete and verified
across macOS, Linux, and Windows.
\red{Remaining work concentrates on (i)~quantitative evaluation of
the perception pipeline on the $13$ held-out photos, and
(ii)~empirical validation of \texttt{/recommend} by simulating
$10^{3}$ battles for original and recommended mixes on each board.
Production integration of the BLR prototype and further inference
batching are noted as longer-term engineering work beyond the
final-report timeline.}

\paragraph{Attribution.}
We use OWL-ViT~\cite{owlvit} weights and the unmodified
\texttt{vit\_small\_patch16\_224} from \texttt{timm}~\cite{timm} as
released; everything else --- the HSV faction heuristic, the
win-rate MLP and its training pipeline, the rules-based combat
simulator, the recommender, the FastAPI service, and the static
HTML frontend --- is implemented from scratch.
A setup audit on a clean Windows host produced the README rewrite,
surfaced three missing \texttt{requirements.txt} entries, and
identified two simulator/recommender bugs; the microbenchmark
harness behind Table~\ref{tab:latency} isolated the
\texttt{find\_best\_attack} bottleneck; a UX audit catalogued
seventeen frontend friction items including the original
\texttt{file://} CORS blocker.
